Masters Research (1)

Abstract: Glycerophosphocholine (GPC) is a common lipid metabolite in eukaryotic organisms. Previous research by Hrach et. al (2026) displays a unique grow mediation response to exposure to exogenous GPC. To understand how GPC contributes to growth inhibition in yeast, we employed a suppressor mutant hunt, exposure experiments, and analysis of genomic data to characterize phenotypes and genotypes of sensitive versus resistant strains.

Methods

This project used a combination of exposure assays, suppressor mutant hunts, WGS, radiolabeling transport assays, computational biology for data analysis, qRT-PCR, and cell viability assays.

Results & Key Takeaways

The results of our studies have determined glycerophosphocholine to be cytotoxic at concentrations above the IC50 and cytostatic at and below. WGS and variant calling data suggested contribution of multiple genes to resistance phenotypes. Transport of the GPC substrate is not impacted in these phenotypes, suggesting a contributing intracellular component. We designed a new approach utilizing laboratory evolution with increasing exposure of GPC to monitor acquisition of mutations overtime. These data will assist with further research to determine molecular underpinnings of GPC exposure.
