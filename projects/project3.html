Undergraduate Research

Abstract: The amphibian chytrid fungus, Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis, is a pathogen that causes chytrimyosis in infected amphibians. This disease has been detrimental to amphibian populations on a pandemic scale, causing extreme reductions in population and even extinction events. A subcategory of infectious disease research aims to understand the ecology and evolution of disease: how a pathogen evolves overtime, its relatedness to other strains and related species, and how it may interact ecologically with other organisms. To understand these topics in the context of amphibians in Western Pennsylvania specifically, we isolated 4 strains of the pathogen from the Pymatuning Reservoir. During my undergraduate research experience at Pitt, I obtained these isolates and performed optimization experiments as well as bacterial metabolite challenge assays. These same isolates continue to be used by the Richards-Zawacki Lab and other colleagues for continued infectious disease and ecology research of the Bd pathogen.

Methods

This project used a combination of sterile isolation, field ecology, animal husbandry, growth optimization experiments, and metabolite challenge assays to further our understanding of Bd.

Results & Key Takeaways

Successful isolation during my undergraduate research program yielded four isolates that I continued to use during my time at Pitt. With exceptional work by the RZ Lab, these isolates have further assisted our understanding of how Bd interacts with the microbiome of amphibians, its relatedness and lineage among isolates, and basic cell biology.
